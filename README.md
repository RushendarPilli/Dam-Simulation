# Computational Simulation of Hydroelectric Dam Systems

An interactive MATLAB simulation that models the structural safety and power output of a hydroelectric dam, combining analytical fluid mechanics with numerical methods: integration, finite differences, derivative-based sensitivity analysis, constrained optimization, and Monte Carlo simulation.

Built as a final project for **ENGT 509 – Applied Computational Methods**.

## Overview

Hydroelectric dams have to balance two competing goals: generate as much power as possible, while keeping the structure safe under the hydrostatic force of the water pushing against it. This project builds a computational model that captures that trade-off and lets you explore it interactively.

Given inputs like water height, dam width, flow rate, turbine efficiency, and dam type, the simulation calculates:

* Hydrostatic pressure and total force on the dam
* Power generated by the turbines
* A safety factor comparing dam strength to the actual force it's under
* How sensitive that force is to changes in water height
* How the system behaves under random real-world variation (Monte Carlo)
* The optimal water height that maximizes power without exceeding a safe force threshold

All of this is wrapped in a MATLAB GUI with live sliders, so you can change any parameter and immediately see how it ripples through the physics, the plots, and the safety status.

## Live Interface

The app has sliders for every input (water height, dam width, flow rate, efficiency, dam type, numerical resolution, and Monte Carlo sample size) and updates all outputs and plots in real time as you drag them.

**Outputs shown:**

* Bottom pressure, total force, power, safety factor, and risk %
* A schematic diagram of the dam with water level and pressure arrows
* Force vs. Height

## The Physics

**Hydrostatic pressure** increases linearly with depth:

```
P = ρ g h
```

**Total force** on the dam face is found by integrating pressure over the height of the dam, which gives a force that grows with the *square* of the water height:

```
F = ½ ρ g H² W
```

**Power output** from the turbines is proportional to flow rate and head height:

```
Power = η ρ g Q H
```

**Safety factor** compares the dam's rated maximum force capacity to the actual force acting on it:

```
SF = F\\\_max / F
```

SF ≥ 2 is treated as safe, 1–2 as a warning zone, and below 1 as unsafe.

## Computational Methods

This is where the project goes beyond a plug-and-chug calculator. Each method below is implemented alongside its analytical counterpart so the numerical results can be validated against theory:

|Method|Purpose|
|-|-|
|**Trapezoidal rule integration** (`trapz`)|Numerically approximates total hydrostatic force and validates the closed-form solution|
|**Derivative-based sensitivity analysis** (`gradient`)|Quantifies how force responds to small changes in water height|
|**Finite difference approximation**|Solves the pressure-depth relationship step by step instead of analytically|
|**Constrained optimization**|Searches across candidate heights to find the one that maximizes power while keeping SF ≥ 1.5|
|**Monte Carlo simulation**|Randomly perturbs height, flow rate, and efficiency across thousands of trials to estimate risk and output variability under real-world uncertainty|
|**Dimensionless validation**|Checks a scaling parameter (π = F / (ρgH²W)) against its expected theoretical value of 0.5 as a sanity check on the whole model|

## Sample Results

At a representative operating point (H = 60 m, W = 100 m, Q = 600 m³/s, η = 0.85, Gravity Dam):

* **Force:** ≈ 1.77 GN
* **Power:** ≈ 300 MW
* **Safety Factor:** ≈ 51 (well within the safe range)
* **Monte Carlo:** power output stayed tightly clustered around 300 MW with negligible unsafe risk under randomized conditions
* **Optimization:** the model suggests a theoretical height of \~150 m could push power to \~750 MW — a useful illustration of the safety/power trade-off, not a real-world recommendation
